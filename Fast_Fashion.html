FAST FASHION

What is fast fashion?
Why is it a problem?

"The more you buy, the more you want.
The more you want, the more you waste."


This describes the world of fast fashion, where clothes and accessories are cheap, allowing you to stay in a culture where mass manufacturers are marketing more than clothing. They are marketing trends designed to whet your desire to shop.

Fast fashion is causing an environmental crisis. The increasingly new demand for more clothing made cheaply and with questionable ethics - both to humanitarian issues and the environment - is cause for concern.

Shopping malls represent an attraction for fast fashion consumers.
Source: Link to Leaders

But what is fast fashion anyway? In order to move past fast fashion and into a more sustainable industry, we all have to understand what exactly we are pushing against. Here are two proposed definitions:

Fast fashion can be defined as cheap, trendy clothing, that samples ideas from the catwalk or celebrity culture and turns them into garments in high street stores at breakneck speed. (Good on You, 2018)

"Fast fashion” is a term used by fashion retailers to describe inexpensive designs that move quickly from the catwalk to stores to meet new trends. As a result of this trend, the tradition of introducing new fashion lines on a seasonal basis is being challenged. Today, it is not uncommon for fast fashion retailers to introduce new products multiple times in a single week to stay on-trend. (Investopedia, 2020)

Up until the mid-twentieth century, the fashion industry ran on 2 cycles a year: Fall/Winter and Spring/Summer. Designers would work many months ahead to plan for each group of seasons and predict what they believed customers would want. Of course, the Industrial Revolution contributed to what we know fashion to be today. It was not as drastic as back then, but around the 1960s the industry started to quicken its pace and lower its costs. Nowadays fast fashion brands produce about 52 “micro-seasons” a year. This means at least one new “collection” every week. According to author Elizabeth Cline, the ever-popular Zara started the craze by shifting to bi-weekly deliveries of new merchandise back in the early aughts. From then on, it has become the norm to have a towering supply of stock at all times.
