Consumerism

A mental health crisis
in the making

"There is no such thing as fast fashion,
just increasingly accelerated consumption."


According to psychologists at the University of Bergen, shopping addiction is a serious issue that affects 1 in 20 Americans. Impulse buying can be hard to control, leading to financial distress. It is easy to get tempted by attractive clothing available at a reasonable price and before long, you are caught in a vicious circle.

It is a known fact that shopping can boost levels of dopamine provided you are in a constant state of desire. And fast fashion ensures that you are. You buy it and once in possession, your dopamine level drops and rises again as you yearn another dress. This harmonic oscillation of dopamine levels can lead to anxiety, depression, and a feeling of worthlessness.

How not to become a victim of fast fashion
Exercise self-control and conscious care for the environment. Keep in mind the harsh working conditions at garment factories. This insightful comment by Hasan Minhaj drives home the point — “The clothes in your suitcase are screwing up the planet more than the flight you put them on.” The fabric used to produce all those trendy clothes consumes too many natural resources and releases toxic chemicals into the environment.

Manufacturing is outsourced to cut costs where the workers are paid peanuts, in order to justify the ridiculously cheap price tags. In cognizance of these debilitating effects of fast fashion, we can do our bit by purchasing only those clothes which we truly require, turning shopping into an occasional rather than a frequent activity, shopping more at thrift shops, and trying to mix and match the clothes you already own to come up with new designs and attire.

An example of more sustainable fashion consumption is thrift shopping.
Source: Bren On The Road

The key is to develop a relationship with the clothes you wear by realising their inherent value instead of just buying and tossing them into your cupboard. Don’t worry too much about repeating an outfit because... no one really cares. Wearing your clothes longer can help reduce your carbon footprint for the garment by 30 percent. Buy good quality, durable, neutral, comfy clothing that lasts long. Be a trend-setter not by sporting new attires but by being a responsible, morally conscious citizen.
