Final Consumer 

Influencers and celebrities like Cardi B post about Fashion Nova on Instagram

“It is not just about clothing,
it is about a disposable society.”


Fast fashion became the new normal. The online retailers' development falls in line with globalization and the logistical efficiency of the 21st century. In the 1950s, if a woman wanted to purchase a ready-made dress, she could spend about $9 (or $72 in today’s dollars) to order an item from a Sears catalog. Today, a shopper could walk into Fashion Nova and buy a simple dress for about $12. The price of an article of clothing today — along with the cost of material, labor, and supply chain logistics required for its creation — is cheap, but it is likely not made to last.


Fashion Nova makes inexpensive
clothes look expensive.

Influencers like Cardi B and the Kardashians drive the fast fashion economy and affect how normal people think about their own clothing choices. “When I’m dressing to go out, I’m dressing to be seen, which is weird to say because I'm not an influencer”, a 20-year-old college student told the New York Times in a story about her shopping habits.

Through visual platforms like Instagram, anyone’s sartorial choices can be scrutinized. Wearing the same outfit twice then starts to seem taboo. According to a 2017 survey commissioned by the London sustainability firm Hubbub, 41 percent of 18- to 25-year-olds feel pressured to wear a different outfit every time they go out. Another survey, commissioned by the Barnado’s charity in 2019, found that British people will spend up to 2.7 billion pounds on clothes in Summer that will only be worn once.

People also tend to prioritize ease of purchase and price of an item over sustainability, according to a 2018 report that surveyed nearly 700 shoppers ages 18 to 37. Fast fashion, then, appears to be the simple solution to appease our desire for novelty. It is much easier to avoid outfit repetition when clothes only cost $20.
