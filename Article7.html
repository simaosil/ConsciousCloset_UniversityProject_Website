The River Ganga

The effects of a harmful
fast fashion industry

"Ganga is a prime example of the unresolved
contradiction between man and the environment."


The effects of the fast fashion industry can be seen in the River Ganga, a major river that flows through Northern India and Bangladesh that is a holy river in Hindu religion. Despite this, the river has been immensely polluted with toxic waste along with the various cities it passes through.

Much of this pollution stems from tanneries in the major city of Kanpur, that dump 50 million liters of toxic waste directly into the river every day. Tanneries are factories where leather is processed and dyed. Many of these tanneries are over 100 years old, with outdated infrastructure and draining systems.

At the time, tanneries used natural and ecological vegetable dyes. However, due to increasing demands for cheaper leather from fast fashion brands, tanneries have switched to chromium, cost-effective but highly toxic. Studies have shown that certain tanneries release up to 100 times the permissible level of chromium into the river.

Foam overflowing from a sewage drain connecting the river Ganga in Kanpur, India.
Source: Reuters Graphics

This pollution of the river has effects beyond the ecosystem of the river, where dead fish, unclaimed human bodies and garbage are dumped. Farmers are not able to use river water to grow their plants, resulting in dying vegetation. This toxic water seeps into the groundwater, poisoning drinking water, and soil. In the village of Jana, a suburb of Kanpur, birth defects, cancer, and jaundice are common. (India Environmental Portal 2019)

“Ganga is a prime example of the unresolved contradiction between man and the environment,” says the protographer Di Sturco. It is a river connected with every aspect of Indian life — a source of water, energy, and livelihood for 500 million people who live along its banks.

A woman crosses a small channel of the Ganga on a submerged bridge made of waste.
Photo by Giulio Di Sturco | Source: National Geographic

This pollution reflects the vast hidden impacts of the fast fashion industry. The leather produced in India may be transported across the world to other countries, where the toxic effects of its creation are not witnessed or felt. Meanwhile, those living in the communities surrounding the tanneries must deal with the effects of the chemical used associated with the demands of the fashion industry.
