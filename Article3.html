Rana Plaza Collapse

The Rana Plaza accident
and its aftermath

"No one should die for fashion."


April 24th 2013 marks the date of the Rana Plaza collapse, where 1,134 people died, and over 2,500 were injured. This devastating incident is the deadliest garment factory accident to date. All for the sake of profit and fashion.

The scene after the Rana Plaza collapse in April 2013
Photograph: Munir Uz Zaman/AFP/Getty Images

Rana Plaza was a building that contained multiple clothing factories. Items were manufactured for the likes of Benetton, Bonmarché, The Children’s Place, Joe Fresh, Monsoon, Accessorize, Mango, Matalan, Primark, Walmart, and Zara. The lower portions of the building also contained shops, a bank, and some apartments. Sadly the building collapsed, killing and injuring many people.

A woman crying and showing a picture of her family member who died on Rana Plaza disaster.
Source: PBS

On April 23rd, 2013, attention was drawn to cracks in Rana Plaza, causing evacuation, closure and a warning to avoid the building. Owner, Sohel Rana, stated the building was safe to return to. Subsequently, factory owners threatened to withhold pay from workers who refused to return to work. Other units in the building remained closed and evacuated. The building collapsed the morning of April 24th, with over 3,000 people inside.

What caused the tragedy of the Rana Plaza collapse?
1. Unsafe building structure. Factories poorly constructed and not designed to hold heavy machinery.

2. Negligence of building owner, Sohel Rana. An instance of placing profits over the value of human safety and life.

3. Pressure from retailers on factories to deliver quickly and cheaply or risk losing business to competitors.

4. Lack of fair living wages. The wages of workers were at the time $35-$60 dollars/month, which is not sustainable.


After the disaster: a fireman carrying a child out of the building.
Source: The Star


Response and aftermath of the Rana Plaza collapse
Workers protested for safer working conditions. Furthermore, 18 garment plants were closed down due to safety precautions. Despite this, factory conditions, in many developing countries, continue to be unsafe.

A new Accord on Factory and Building Safety in Bangladesh was created. However, a number of high street retailers refused to sign it. In 2011, reforms that would have retailers pay more for apparel, to help Bangladesh factories improve safety standards, were also rejected. It’s no surprise that factories, not only in Bangladesh, are still under pressure to deliver.

Following the Rana Plaza collapse, global consumers continued to express their anger at the lack of responsibility of retailers. Some have spoken out against the abuse of human rights within fashion. Today, we see people remembering the tragedy through Fashion Revolution, an organisation who has created the campaign #WhoMadeYourClothes to fight for a cleaner, safer, fairer, more transparent and accountable fashion industry.
